Build Map · Phase 4

The field pilot —
ground truth or it didn't happen

Everything before this phase happened on our terms: staged hazards, cooperative testers, controlled light. The pilot puts the prototype on real heads at a real site for four weeks and measures it against what a human safety observer actually saw. It ends with numbers and a signature, not a vibe.

4 weeks on-site Needs a signed pilot site Ends in a signed report
The rule that governs everything on site

Augment, never replace

For the entire pilot, Hazard Watch is a supplementary layer. Every existing safety control — spotters, permits, LOTO, toolbox talks — runs exactly as before. No procedure is relaxed because "the glasses are watching," no alert substitutes for a human judgment, and any incident follows the site's normal protocol first, ours second. This isn't just ethics; it's what makes a site willing to sign, and it keeps the pilot's failure modes survivable while the numbers are still unproven.

Before day one

the pilot doesn't start until every box is checked

Program inputs

  • Phases 1–3 passed — slice demo, model at recall floor, judgment layer through its wear-test
  • Pilot site signed — one site, named site sponsor, agreed hazard focus (which Tier 1 classes matter there)
  • Consent pack executed — every document in the pack below, signed before any capture
  • Safety SME committed — owns the ground-truth observation plan and the final sign-off

Logistics

  • Crew recruited — 3–5 volunteer workers, mixed roles, opt-in with the right to withdraw at any time
  • Hardware kit — one glasses + phone pair per worker, plus one spare pair and charging plan
  • Tester channel live — pilot build distributed via Meta's invite-only release channel
  • Daily data path — event logs pulled to a review machine each shift end (files, no cloud dependency)

Four weeks, three modes

shadow → live → prove
Week 1
SHADOW MODE

The system watches, workers see nothing

Full pipeline runs, every detection and judgment is logged — but no alerts render. Meanwhile the safety observer independently logs real hazards on paper. This week produces the baseline: what the site's actual hazard rate is, and what the model would have said about it, with zero risk of alert fatigue poisoning the pilot on day two.

  • Ground-truth log vs. shadow detections → field recall & false-alarm baseline per class
  • Battery, comfort, and connectivity reality-check across full shifts
  • Threshold + rate-cap retune on real data before anyone hears an alert
Weeks 2–3
LIVE ALERTS

Alerts on, tuning loop running

Workers get real alerts, tuned from week 1's baseline. Every evening: logs pulled, misses and false alarms reviewed against the observer's record, hard frames labeled and queued for the retrain loop. One mid-point model/policy update ships at the end of week 2 — versioned, so before/after is measurable.

  • Daily triage: every critical-class miss reviewed within 24 h
  • Weekly worker debriefs — 15 min, structured, anonymous-comment option
  • Phase 3 trust metrics tracked per worker per shift (ack latency, mute events, agreement)
Week 4
SIGN-OFF RUNS

Frozen build, measured week

No more tuning. The final build runs a full week untouched while the observer logs ground truth. This week's numbers — not the tuned-daily weeks — are the pilot's reported results. Ends with the SME's structured sign-off review and the worker exit interviews.

  • All pass-bar metrics measured on the frozen build only
  • SME failure-mode review: every miss, every false alarm, dispositioned
  • Exit interviews: would this crew keep wearing it? Verbatims for the report

The consent & privacy pack

six documents, signed before any capture

A POV camera on a worker records colleagues who never opted in. The pack exists to make that legitimate, and drafting it starts now — legal review is the longest lead-time item in this phase, and in works-council jurisdictions it can be the longest in the program.

P1

Site agreement

Scope, duration, hazard focus, the augment-never-replace clause, termination rights, and who owns the pilot report.

P2

Worker opt-in

Plain-language consent for wearers: what's captured, what's inferred, right to withdraw any time without consequence.

P3

Bystander policy

Zone signage, crew briefing for non-wearers, capture-indicator explanation, and how a colleague objects.

P4

Data handling

On-device processing statement, what leaves the phone (labeled frames only, reviewed), retention clock, deletion path.

P5

No-surveillance clause

Explicit: no individual identification, no face recognition, no performance monitoring, no disciplinary use — ever.

P6

Incident protocol

If the system misses or misleads around a real event: site protocol first, preserved logs, joint review, disclosure duties.

Who's on the ground

The pilot team

  • ▸Pilot lead (us) — on site weeks 1 and 4, daily triage remotely in between.
  • ▸Safety observer — the site's own safety person or our SME embedded; owns the ground-truth log, blind to model output during shifts.
  • ▸Site sponsor — clears access, schedules crews, first escalation point.
  • ▸ML engineer (remote) — nightly triage, labels hard frames, ships the week-2 update.

What the pilot ships

  • ✓The pilot report — pass-bar table, failure-mode dispositions, SME signature. This is the document sales and investors get.
  • ✓Model + policy v2 — retrained on real POV footage; the flywheel is now running on customer data.
  • ✓The consent pack as a template — reusable per site from now on.
  • ✓Case-study raw material — verbatims, before/after numbers, footage cleared for use.
Exit criteriaWeek-4 frozen-build numbers hit the pass bar, zero safety regressions, the SME signs the failure-mode review, and a majority of the crew would keep wearing it. Any critical-class miss without a disposition blocks sign-off.
Why this phase is the one that matters commercially. Everything before it produces engineering confidence; this phase produces the three assets a safety product actually sells on — a signed report with field numbers, a reference customer who kept the system after the pilot, and proprietary training data from real work. If the pass bar isn't hit, the report still ships: it says exactly which classes fell short and by how much, and the program loops those classes back through Phase 2 with a month of real footage in hand. Either way, the flywheel is turning.

Pass-bar targets are planning defaults to be ratified with the site sponsor and SME before week 1 — the site's own risk profile may raise them. Ground-truth comparison assumes an observer log; where the site runs existing incident/near-miss reporting, reconcile against that too. Works-council / GDPR jurisdictions: pack review can take 4–8 weeks — start before Phase 3 completes.
